JA Company Code of Ethics

Business Ethics: The ability to make decisions that are not attached to financial gain alone.

Code of Ethics: A set of rules or guidelines outlining ethical principles that govern decisions and behavior — how employees should behave, following the law, reporting unethical conduct, being environmentally conscious, sourcing ethically made supplies, and being inclusive.

"How would we feel if everyone knew about the decision we are making?"

— Test of Disclosure

PLUS Model for Decision Making

  • P = Policies & Procedures — Does this align with company policies?
  • L = Legal — Does this violate any laws or regulations?
  • U = Universal — Is this in line with core values and company culture?
  • S = Self — Does it meet my standards of fairness and honesty?

Product & Service Guidelines

Should:

  • Be something customers are willing to pay for
  • Be something members are passionate about

Should NOT:

Group Product/Service Mastermind

Step 1: Write as many life or business problems as you can.

Step 2: Take one problem and write as many solutions (either a product or service) to that problem.

Step 3: Pick one solution to one of the problems. Then create a product or service and write a 60–90 second pitch about:

  • What your product is
  • What its name is
  • What your target audience is
  • How much it costs
  • Why you should pick them

A norm is an informal guideline about what is considered normal social behavior in a particular group.

5 Stages of Group Development:

  • Storming
  • Norming
  • Performing
  • Adjourning

Real Company Norms

"Productivity is Key"

Building Your Team Norms

Think about these categories:

  • Decisions — How do we make decisions together?

Decision-Making Methods

  • Unanimous: Everyone is in complete agreement.
  • Consensus: May not be everyone's first preference, but all can give consent. Relies on problem-solving, empathy, and self-sacrifice.
  • Stalemate: Even draw — further discussion required to move forward.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR is a voluntary approach that businesses take to meet or exceed shareholder expectations. "Doing well by doing good."

Old School

  • Profit
  • Revenue
  • Legal compliance

New School

  • Social impact
  • Environmental sustainability
  • Ethical practices

Modern Trends: Deliver the good, not just the goods. Pursue values, not just value. Help make the world a better place.

Triple Bottom Line

Companies setting standards where the bottom line is about more than just money:

  • Financial Performance — Be profitable (legally)
  • Environmental Sustainability — Reduce, reuse, recycle
  • Social Responsibility — Be a community participant

CSR Examples

  • Tim Hortons — Involved in fair trade, sponsors sports and community initiatives
  • TOMS Shoes — "One for One" — every purchase helps a person in need
  • Tentree — Partnered with non-profits to restore ecosystems and employ local communities

SMART Goals

Write a SMART goal related to what you want to get out of Company Program:

  • S = Specific — What exactly are you going to achieve? How detailed can you get?
  • M = Measurable — What tangible evidence will show accomplishment? How will you know you succeeded?
  • A = Attainable — Is the effort and commitment needed realistic? Can you envision the results?
  • R = Relevant — Why is your goal really important to you? How will you persevere when it gets hard?
  • T = Timely — When will you cross the finish line? What pace will you set?

Business Model Canvas

The Business Model Canvas is a tool that helps outline the details of a business. It provides structure and delivers:

  • Focus — everyone will be on the same page
  • Flexibility — easy to tweak the model and try new things
  • Transparency — the team will have an easier time understanding the business model

Lean Business Start-Up

Three important steps in creating a successful venture:

  • BUILD — Form the company, create a team, make a product or service
  • MEASURE — How many sales? How efficient is production? How effective are employees?
  • LEARN — What can we take from what we've measured? How do we improve?

A successful company is one that is constantly re-evaluating itself. By following BUILD-MEASURE-LEARN, you can adapt more quickly!

Venture Funding

JA Companies typically gain start-up funding by selling shares to investors.

Shares = Units of ownership in a company | Equity = Investors take ownership to help the business start

Pros

  • Shared risk
  • Access to capital
  • No obligation to pay back initial investment

Cons

  • Profit sharing
  • Pressure for growth
  • Loss of control

JA Company Shares

Teams value shares between $5-$20 and can raise a maximum of $300.

Common Shares (Required):

  • Required for all members
  • Participate in profit share
  • Voting privileges

Preferred Shares (Optional):

  • Not eligible for profit share
  • No voting privileges
  • Face value + 20% dividend

Other Ways to Raise Capital

Bootstrapping

Personal Investments

Use your own money or assets. Keep 100% control and profits, but may limit starting capital.

Example: Mailchimp — valued at $12B when acquired by Intuit

Gift Capital & Crowdfunding

Family, Friends, Kickstarter

Money gifted/loaned by people you know, or raised from a large pool online.

Example: Exploding Kittens — raised $8M on Kickstarter

Debt Funding

Credit & Loans

Bank loans or credit cards. Quick access but higher interest charges.

Example: G Adventures (JA Alumnus Bruce Poon Tip) — started with credit cards

Angel & Venture Capitalists

Investors

Wealthy individuals or firms who invest directly. May provide mentorship and connections.

Example: Shopify — $250K angel investment in 2008, now multibillion-dollar

Business Incubators

Accelerators

Provide space, admin resources, mentoring, and higher rate of success.

Example: Ritual — supported by Ryerson's DMZ

Grants & Subsidies

Government Funding

May not need to pay back. Encourages innovation but competitive and conditional.

Example: CarbonCure Technologies — SDTC program funding
